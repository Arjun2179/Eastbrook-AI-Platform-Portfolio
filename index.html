Overview

Project Overview

Eastbrook Youth AI Well-Being turns a silent classroom risk into a measurable, intervenable problem.

Powered by React, Node.js, and PostgreSQL (Neon), the solution covers 400 students across 12,000 structured observations, 34 data fields, and two study phases — AS-IS (baseline) and TO-BE (modeled intervention). Three role-based interfaces give students, educators, and analysts the right visibility at the right time.

💡 Modeled Intervention Outcomes (TO-BE phase):
• Verification rate: 56.0% → 66.7% (+10.7 pts)
• Automation bias: 44.0% → 33.3% (−10.7 pts)
• Daily screen time: 8.6 hrs → 7.8 hrs (−0.8 hrs)
• Nudge success rate measured at 25.6% positive effect

⚡ Challenge

The Problem: A Broken Checkpoint

Eastbrook's workflow has five steps. The failure point is step 3 — the verification checkpoint students skip under pressure while the school sees the risk too late to intervene.

🤖 Extreme Prompt Volume

14,119 AI prompts per day across 400 students. 35.3 prompts per student per day — routine, high-frequency AI dependence.

⚠️ Weak Verification

Only 56% of AI outputs are independently checked. 44% accept without verification. At high task complexity, verification drops to 40.4%.

😣 Physical Strain

8.6 hours screen time per day. Eye dryness at 7.1/10. Neck pain at 5.9/10. The harm is not only cognitive — it is physical and chronic.

🕐 Late Educator Visibility

Staff see behavioral patterns reactively, after chronic risk has developed. There is no real-time signal for who needs a nudge today.

Who Is Affected

👩‍🎓

Students

Directly affected by prompt volume, low verification, screen strain, and social-emotional AI use. The system is designed to protect both learning independence and well-being.

👩‍🏫

Educators

Need earlier visibility into who is struggling, who needs nudges, and which behavior patterns signal rising dependence or physical strain.

🏥

Counselors & Support Staff

Relevant once the case extends beyond coursework into emotional support, serious-topic AI use, and safeguarding — 33.2% of over-reliant students discuss serious topics with AI.

🏛️

District & Policy Stakeholders

Need evidence for healthier AI governance, device-use guidelines, and intervention policies that do not rely on blanket bans.

The Core Risk: 23 dominant over-reliant students show 36.0% verification and 68.4% accept-without-checking. Inside that subgroup: 41.6% use AI for social messaging, 13.6% share PII. This is a safeguarding case, not just an academic productivity issue.

🎯 Solution

Eastbrook AI Platform

Layered architecture connecting business workflows, application services, and secure cloud delivery

One architecture view ties together the top-layer business, IS, and technology architecture with the bottom-layer services, API contracts, and stack choices that implement it.

🏗️ Architecture Reading Guide

The diagram makes the platform traceable from business capability, to application service, to security and hosting, and finally down to the exact tables and representative routes that make the experience work.

Business Architecture

Student AI behavior tracking, AI verification monitoring, physical wellness monitoring, educator risk intervention, policy and compliance analysis, and analyst KPI reporting.

IS Architecture

Node.js REST API with Express.js, a Python analytics engine for synthetic dataset processing, Neon PostgreSQL, and a React 19 + TypeScript UI built with Vite, React Router DOM v7, Recharts, and Lucide React Icons.

Tech. Architecture

Vercel Cloud, Vercel Edge CDN, Vercel Serverless Functions, SPA routing, JWT role-based auth, Helmet.js, CORS, express-rate-limit, bcryptjs, and environment-variable-based deployment configuration.

Operational Contracts

The architecture names eight core tables and concrete public, student, educator, and analyst routes, so the role-based experience is grounded in explicit database and API contracts rather than a generic dashboard concept.

Bottom-Layer Business Services

Student Session Logging, Educator Nudge System, Risk Alert Engine, Training Module Delivery, and AS-IS vs TO-BE Comparison are presented as the business-facing services enabled by the platform.

Named Data Foundations

The architecture explicitly calls out profiles, student_day_metrics, student_sessions, training_modules, training_progress, risk_alerts, nudges, and educator_student_map.

Role-Based API Surface

Public routes support overview and comparison, student routes support dashboard and training, educator routes support nudges and trends, and analyst routes support KPI monitoring and evaluation.

🔷 Business Services Mapped from the Architecture

1️⃣ Student Session Logging

Anchored by POST /api/student/sessions
Students record AI usage sessions that feed the student_sessions and student_day_metrics layer used for prompt volume, verification, and behavior tracking.

2️⃣ Educator Nudge System

Driven by POST /api/educator/nudges
Educators can send targeted nudges based on behavior and wellness signals instead of waiting for end-of-cycle reporting.

3️⃣ Risk Alert Engine

Backed by risk_alerts and educator_student_map
The intervention model turns daily signals into routed alerts so educators see risk when it is actionable, not after it becomes chronic.

4️⃣ Training Module Delivery

Implemented through training_modules and training_progress
Students move through guidance content while the platform keeps track of completion state and readiness for follow-up support.

5️⃣ AS-IS vs TO-BE Comparison

Served through GET /api/public/comparison
The architecture explicitly preserves the comparison view as a public-facing capability for showing modeled KPI deltas across the two study phases.

6️⃣ Role Routing & Secure Access

React Router DOM v7 + JWT role-based auth
Public, student, educator, and analyst routes stay separated while Helmet.js, CORS, express-rate-limit, bcryptjs, and environment variables harden the deployment.

📊 Data

Data Architecture

Synthetic but deliberately structured — one row per student per day, designed to make AI behavior, health signals, and intervention logic measurable together

Why Synthetic Data?

🔒

Privacy First

No real student identities exposed. FERPA and GDPR compliant from day one. Safe for demonstrations, analysis, and academic research.

🧪

Realistic Behavior Patterns

Mimics actual student AI usage patterns. Phase-controlled design separates AS-IS from TO-BE. Enables before-after comparison with the same KPI framework.

📈

Built for Analysis

34 fields per row captures AI prompts, verification rates, strain metrics, reliance type, safeguarding signals, and nudge response — all in one structure.

Dataset Overview

400 Students Four reliance types: appropriate, over, under, variable
12,000 Total Observations 6,000 AS-IS + 6,000 TO-BE rows (15 days × 400 students)
34 Fields per Row AI metrics, health scores, reliance type, safeguarding flags
14,119 Daily Prompts (AS-IS) 35.3 prompts per student per day at baseline
23 High-Risk Students Dominant over-reliant subgroup — 36.0% verification, 68.4% accept-without-check
8.6 hrs Daily Screen Time Eye dryness 7.1/10 · Neck pain 5.9/10 at baseline
Analytics-side ERD for the synthetic study dataset — USER ↔ USER_DAY with phase, complexity, and reliance-type dimensions. In the live prototype architecture, these analytics outputs are then surfaced through operational app tables such as profiles, student_sessions, and risk_alerts.

Dashboard & Key Evidence

Three charts from the AS-IS analysis that anchor the project's evidence base

Evidence #1: Setup — Extreme Daily Volume

"Students maintain extremely high daily AI interactions (>14,000 prompts/day)"

  • 14,119 total prompts per day across 400 students
  • 35.3 prompts per student per day — routine, sustained usage
  • Consistent across all 15 observation days — no sign of self-regulation
  • Volume alone does not indicate harm — but combined with low verification, it becomes a risk signal
Why this matters:
"High prompt volume at low verification is the worst combination — students are offloading cognitive effort to AI at scale, but not checking the outputs."

Baseline: 14,119/day | TO-BE modeled: 11,871/day (−2,248)

Evidence #2: Conflict — Stagnant Verification

"Despite high usage, independent fact-checking remains stagnant at ~56%"

  • 56.0% average verification rate — barely above the midpoint
  • Flat trend across 15 days — students do not self-correct
  • At high complexity tasks: verification drops to 40.4%
  • 23 dominant over-reliant students: only 36.0% verification
The intervention target:
"Verification is the core KPI lever. Zhang et al. (2024) confirm: AI learning outcomes improve when users actively check AI outputs."

AS-IS: 56.0% | TO-BE modeled: 66.7% (+10.7 pts)

Evidence #3: Conflict — Persistent Physical Strain

"Sustained unmoderated usage drives severe, persistent physical strain"

  • Eye dryness: 7.1/10 average — well above comfortable threshold
  • Neck pain: 5.9/10 average — chronic postural strain
  • Both metrics are flat across 15 days — no natural recovery
  • This is not a productivity problem — it is a student health problem
Health framing:
"Rajani et al. (2022) and NIPH (2024) connect sustained screen exposure directly to the severity levels measured in this dataset."

Eye AS-IS: 7.1 | TO-BE: 6.3  ·  Neck AS-IS: 5.9 | TO-BE: 5.3

🎯 Key Dashboard Insights

Insight #1: Complexity Kills Verification

The Finding: Verification drops from 66.0% at low complexity to 53.6% at medium and 40.4% at high complexity. The risk intensifies exactly where independent checking is most critical.

Why it matters: High-stakes work (complex assignments) is where students most need to verify — and where they least do so. Educators should prioritise nudges at high-complexity task moments.

Action: Trigger nudges at assignment complexity thresholds

Insight #2: 23 Students Drive Disproportionate Risk

The Finding: The dominant over-reliant subgroup of 23 students shows 36.0% verification and 68.4% accept-without-checking. Within this group: 41.6% use AI for social messaging, 33.2% discuss serious topics, 13.6% share PII.

Why it matters: Targeted intervention on 23 students (5.75% of cohort) would address the highest-concentration risk — safeguarding, not just academic performance.

Action: Escalate 23-student subgroup to counsellors immediately

Insight #3: Physical Strain is Already Chronic

The Correlation: Average daily screen time is 8.6 hours. Eye dryness averages 7.1/10 and neck pain 5.9/10 across all 15 observation days with no recovery signal.

  • This is not acute — it is chronic and embedded
  • Physical harm tracks directly with prompt volume and screen time
  • TO-BE modeled breaks: screen time drops to 7.8 hrs
Action: Enforce break nudges at 60-minute continuous-use thresholds

🔒 Ethics

Ethics & Responsible Design

🔐 Privacy & PII Protection

PII-sharing risk is part of the harm model — 13.6% of over-reliant students share PII with AI. The platform treats privacy as a first-class concern, not an afterthought. Synthetic dataset = zero real student identities exposed.

🛡️ Safeguarding for Minors

Serious-topic AI use (33.2%) and emotional support requests mean the system must be framed as a student-safety tool, not merely a productivity dashboard. APA and UNICEF guidance informs the child-centered design.

⚖️ Avoiding Overclaiming

The TO-BE phase represents modeled intervention logic, not completed live deployment. The portfolio is careful to frame outcomes as "projected" and "modeled" — not "achieved." All numbers are sourced from the verified dataset only.

🔍 Transparency & Responsible Tracking

Students should understand what is being tracked and why — not to ban AI use, but to support healthier habits, earlier intervention, and genuine learning independence. Monitoring is a support tool, not surveillance.

🎯 Results

AS-IS vs TO-BE: The Case for Intervention

✅ What the Project Demonstrates

  • Validated Concept: Proved that combined AI behavior + health + reliance data can model intervention impact in a structured dataset

  • Bias Reduction: Automation bias drops from 44.0% to 33.3% when micro-verification prompts are active
  • Physical Relief: Modeled breaks reduce screen time by 0.8 hrs, eye dryness by 0.8 pts, neck pain by 0.6 pts

💡 The Case Expanded While We Built It

Started as a usage-tracking idea. Became a combined verification, health, and educator-intervention case once the dataset was analysed. Scope decisions shaped everything.

📊 Data Design Shaped Product Design

Because the dataset captured prompts, verification, strain, and reliance together, the solution had to become role-based rather than single-user reporting.

🎯 Accuracy Over Style

A major lesson: unify all numbers under one source hierarchy before designing the final portfolio. Drift from earlier drafts costs more time than it saves.

🔬 Next Steps Are Practical

The next stage: live pilot testing, outcome validation with real school data, and refinement of nudge thresholds, safeguarding workflows, and intervention timing.

🚧 Challenges & Solutions

Scope Creep: Dataset kept revealing new risks → MoSCoW prioritisation → focused on verification, strain, and safeguarding subgroup as core deliverables
Number Drift: Early drafts had inconsistent metrics → created METRIC_SOURCE_OF_TRUTH.json as single authoritative source for all KPIs
Overclaiming Risk: TO-BE phase tempting to over-interpret → strict framing as "modelled intervention outcomes" throughout all deliverables
Architecture Complexity: 3 interfaces × 3 roles → clear RACI matrix + separate role-gated routes enforced clean separation
